Multi-Scale Global-Instance Prompt Tuning for Continual Test-Time Adaptation in Medical Image Segmentation

Lingrui Li1, Yanfeng Zhou2, Nan Pu3*, Xin Chen1*, Zhun Zhong1,4

*Corresponding Authors
Paper Code

MGIPT addresses continual test-time adaptation via complementary Adaptive-scale Instance Prompts (AIP) and Multi-scale Global Prompts (MGP), achieving superior performance with minimal performance degradation across continually changing target domains.

Abstract

Distribution shift is a common challenge in medical images obtained from different clinical centers, significantly hindering the deployment of pre-trained semantic segmentation models in real-world applications across multiple domains. Continual Test-Time Adaptation (CTTA) has emerged as a promising approach to address cross-domain distribution shifts during continually evolving target domains. Most existing CTTA methods rely on incrementally updating model parameters, which inevitably suffer from error accumulation and catastrophic forgetting, especially in long-term adaptation.

Recent prompt-tuning-based works have shown potential to mitigate these issues by updating only visual prompts. While these approaches have demonstrated promising performance, several limitations remain: (1) lacking multi-scale prompt diversity, (2) inadequate incorporation of instance-specific knowledge, and (3) risk of privacy leakage.

To overcome these limitations, we propose Multi-scale Global-Instance Prompt Tuning (MGIPT), to enhance scale diversity of prompts as well as capture both global- and instance-level knowledge for robust CTTA. Specifically, MGIPT consists of an Adaptive-scale Instance Prompt (AIP) that dynamically learns lightweight and instance-specific prompts to mitigate error accumulation with adaptive optimal-scale selection, and a Multi-scale Global-level Prompt (MGP) that captures domain-level knowledge across different scales to ensure robust adaptation with anti-forgetting capabilities. Extensive experiments on medical image segmentation benchmarks demonstrate that MGIPT outperforms state-of-the-art methods, achieving robust adaptation across continually changing target domains with particularly strong performance in long-term CTTA scenarios.

Method Overview

MGIPT leverages low-frequency prompt tuning with two complementary components, combined through confidence-weighted ensemble at inference:

(1) Adaptive-scale Instance Prompt (AIP): Each test image is transformed to the frequency domain via FFT, combined with a lightweight instance-level prompt, and optimized using Progressive Prompt Tuning (PPT) with an Early Stopping (ES) strategy. Starting from a 1×1 prompt and iteratively expanding the receptive field, AIP finds the optimal prompt scale for each individual sample, preventing overfitting while capturing fine-grained instance variations.

(2) Multi-scale Global Prompt (MGP): Three global prompts of distinct sizes (bs−2, bs, bs+2) are maintained to capture domain-level knowledge across varying frequency scales. A Teacher-Student mechanism with Exponential Moving Average (EMA) updates enables knowledge accumulation without requiring a memory bank, improving privacy preservation and reducing error accumulation.

Inference: BN calibration fuses source and target statistics, while confidence-based weights dynamically combine predictions from all four prompts (1 instance + 3 global) for the final output.

Qualitative Results

Qualitative comparison on the Optic Disc/Cup (OD/OC) segmentation benchmark. MGIPT produces more accurate and complete segmentation masks across target domains compared to existing CTTA methods.

Visualization of fine-tuned instance and global prompts and their adapted images on the OD/OC task. Multi-scale global prompts (GP) are generally similar across domains, while instance prompts (IP) capture unique characteristics of each image, showing their complementary nature.

Performance over 3 continual rounds of adaptation on OD/OC datasets. MGIPT maintains superior performance with minimal degradation (PD = 0.07), significantly outperforming methods that suffer from catastrophic forgetting.

Quantitative Results

On the Optic Disc/Cup segmentation benchmark, MGIPT achieves the best average DSC of 72.35% across all five target domains, surpassing the strongest competing method (MoASE) by +1.20% DSC. Notably, MGIPT shows particularly strong anti-forgetting capability in long-term CTTA with a performance degradation of only 0.07% after 3 rounds, compared to 5.12% for TENT and 1.44% for CoTTA.

On the Polyp segmentation benchmark, MGIPT achieves the best average DSC of 80.64%, Emax of 89.45%, and Sα of 85.71%, outperforming all competing methods across all four target domains and metrics.
